Music Background

This is the section where I will talk about my music background in and out of the classroom. I have done a great deal of performing in my past and would like to talk about all of it here. I strongly believe that music is one of the best creative outlets an engineer can have, and I am very grateful for all of the opportunities that I have had simply because I learned how to play the trombone.

Theory Background

Music theory is not for the weak. When signing up for Music Theory I at Purdue I was thinking "I've played an instrument for 10 years of my life, this should be an easy A elective!" I was wrong. Anyway, I took it because I wanted to get better at writing my own arrangements, and I needed the credit. We learned about everything, from simple rhythm notation to harmonic triads to the functional harmonic paradigm and root position part writing. The final project for the course was a chorale written in the style of Bach, which is my one and only original composition.

This class gave me a firm understanding of everything related to tonal music, which will be beneficial to my hobbyist level arranging. I have yet to write any arrangements since finishing the class, but I look forward to it now that I am more knowledgeable in at least *some* music theory.

No, I will not be taking Music Theory II, as I value my sanity and it is just a hobby.

Bach Chorale

This is the only place on this page where I discuss actual music theory, don't worry if it scares you as much as it scares me.

Here is my only composition, the root position Bach style chorale discussed in the above theory section. It is written in a SATB format, and I am especially proud of the third phrase, where the alto and tenor voices trade this eighth-eighth-quarter rhythm back and forth while the bass and soprano repeat the same rhythm twice around them. The chorale also makes use of a harmonic progression called deceptive motion. Basically a V chord strongly wants to resolve to the I chord, because it contains the leading tone as the third, and it involves a root motion of a perfect fifth. Deceptive motion, however, is when the V chord moves to the vi chord, which usually surprises the listener, as we are so used to the V to I resolve.

Unfortunately, I will not publish the actual score, as I am not sure if that will violate the syllabus for academic integrity reasons. Not sure how many future Theory I students will be digging through this website, but it is better safe then sorry.

Arrangements

This is the section where I will discuss all of my arrangements that I have made or that I am working on. I don't have many yet, but I plan to make a ton more in the future. I use Musescore 4 to write all of my arrangements, however some of my older ones were done in Musescore 3.

"Rosebud" by Man-Made Sunshine

This song is one of the most beautiful songs ever written in my opinion. Written and performed by Conor Mason, the lead singer of the modern alternative rock band Nothing But Thieves, it is the first ballad that I have ever arranged. It has a very somber melody and lyrics, as it (I think) is about being left by your partner when you're willing to work things out but they aren't interested. Regardless, Conor is one of the most spectacular vocalists of all time and I am very grateful to exist on this planet at the same time as him.

I am still working on this one, but here is the original song so you can see what I am talking about.

I have completed the first half of the song, which is a much more acoustic, piano-based portion. I have also begun the second half which has a much more indie alternative feel, with lots of vocal effects and synthesized instrumentation. I am very excited to get to the final chorus, as it is just as powerful as it is beautiful, and I hope to be able to captivate that in my arrangement.

Another thing that excites me about this arrangement is that it is one of the first that I am making some of my own creative decisions. In the past, I have mostly just been transcribing the song directly into a score without adding anything. In this arrangement however, I am adding some of my own ideas. For example, after the first chorus, I move the main melody to the bass voices, which is something that I have never chosen to do, and I really like how it sounds! I will publish this one and put the link here when I finish it.

"APT" by ROSÃ‰ and Bruno Mars

Yeah, yeah you probably hate this song. I think its catchy and it got stuck in my head so I made an arrangement of it. It is the first arrangement I had ever written with more than 4 parts, as it is a trombone ensemble arrangement meant for 8 trombones. It is also the only arrangement of mine that has ever been performed in front of an audience (or played by a real ensemble at all for that matter). I wrote it for a talent show fundraiser for a band honor society at Purdue, and it went very well.

You can find it published here on Musescore.

Overall, I am decently proud of this arrangement, but I think there were also many things that I could have done better. I was in a bit of a haste to complete it when I wrote the second half of it, so there are some notation errors and the balance between parts is a little off. I don't think I will revisit this one though, as I have other scores I want to work on.

"Knee Socks" by The Arctic Monkeys

Out of all of the arrangements that I have completed, this is the one that I am the most proud of. It is a trombone quartet, but with divisis included can be played by 9 people without any complete overlap. This song was one of my favorites when I arranged it. Not only is the guitar riff catchy, the overall song is very upbeat but has a sort of contradictory meloncholy, nostalgic feeling. I don't know how to put it to words but it makes me feel like I want something but I don't know what that thing that I want is.

You can find it published here on Musescore.

There are certainly some rhythms that I got wrong (my dictation skills have improved in the years since I arranged this), but as I said, this is my proudest arrangement. I think I nailed the meloncholic feeling I described before while also writing parts that would actually be fun to play. The biggest problem with the arrangement is that it is pretty high in terms of range, so it is probably exclusive to intermediate or advanced players.

"Black Holes (Solid Ground)" by The Blue Stones

This is a great song from the Canadian blues-rock duo The Blue Stones. It is a very simple but effective song, which is why I was able to arrange it for a trombone quartet in one night. I am pretty proud of the arrangement, however, as I think it is the most playable quartet that I have written.

You can find it published here on Musescore.

Like I said, I believe this to be my most playable quartet, as in it has no ridiculous range restrictions or rhythms. The greatest limiting factor is the less common key signature for a trombonist, but any key signature is managable with enough practice. There are some things that I would change about it if I hadn't already published it, but I think I will leave it as it is to work on other things instead.

"Typhoons" by Royal Blood

Those who know me know that if Royal Blood has 1000 fans, I am one of them. If they have 1 fan, its me, and if they have 0 fans, I must be dead. Anyway, I had to arrange a song by them, and Typhoons is one of my all time favorites. It fits that category of "really upbeat songs about really sad topics", as it is a description of the many complex emotions that come with a mental health struggle, but it has a sick bass riff and a driving disco-rock feel.

Another interesting thing about this arrangement is the way I had to try to mimic the band's iconic sound. Royal Blood is a British rock duo consisting of a drummer and a bassist/vocalist/pianist. No guitar. At all (except a few songs). You would think that there is a guitar, but what you hear when you listen to Royal Blood is a very heavily distorted bass guitar, that almost sounds like a guitar and a bass playing at the same time.

You can find it published here on Musescore.

I think I did an ok job mimicing the bass sound by doubling the riff by octave between parts, but I also feel like I was missing something with this arrangement. This was my second ever arrangement, so I am happy with it nonetheless, but some parts of it feel a little empty. If I had arranged this one today, I would have figured out some sort of countermelody to add to it, but at this point of my arranging career I was too scared to do anything but transcribe what I could hear.

"R U Mine?" by The Arctic Monkeys

This was my very first arrangement. I started arranging because I would hear songs that I wanted to play, but there would never be any parts for me to play because most music composers, arrangers, etc don't commonly go through an Arctic Monkeys phase. So I took it into my own hands and did it myself. What I love about this song, other than the riff and the lyrics that make no sense, is the brief solo section.

You can find it published here on Musescore.

As mentioned above, I love the solo section of this piece, and I am proud of how I arranged it. I made sure that all 4 parts had a solo so that the melody gets passed around between all players. I think this was a really good arrangement for my first and is still one of my favorite arrangements that I have written. There are of course things that I would do differently now, but I think I will leave this one as a time capsule.
