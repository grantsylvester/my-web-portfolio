Minecraft Redstone Tomfoolery

In case you were not aware, the game Minecraft has a fully fleshed-out electrical system called redstone. In the game, it is usually used for small practical things such as a button to open a door or a lever to turn on the lights in your base. If you are without limitations in creative mode, however, it becomes much easier to create much larger, real world circuitry.

Growing up, this always seemed too hard to figure out, so I just played the game like a normal person instead of learning digital system design as a 10 year old (biggest mistake of my life if you ask me). Now, however, I am unemployed and have learned how things like Systemverilog and RTL design work, so I thought I would give it a shot.

The first thing that I did was learn some basic logic gates. These are not too hard, and usually only take up 2-blocks of space. The hardest part of redstone is the fact that if you place dust (wire) next to more dust, it WILL connect, so you really have to take advantage of the third dimension when designing circuits.

D Flip-Flop

The next thing I created was a digital flip-flop, and I wanted to do it with nothing but a gate level schematic. There are lots of nifty and compact ways to make a lot of components, but I wanted to do it the primitive way first. I constructed it with only nor gates and much to my surprise, was successful on the first try. The output would only match the input if the enable signal was high and would otherwise remain at its original value.

8-Bit Register

After storing one bit, I thought I would store 8. Now, however, I ditched my primitive, gate-level flip-flop and found an online example of a compact and stackable one. These work by using hoppers with outputs connected in a feedback loop. A redstone comparator checks if the hopper on the outside contains an item (1) or if it is empty (0). With clever block placement, you can make it so that the hoppers only transfer items if the enable signal is high, just like a flip-flop. This design is stackable, so all it takes to make an 8 bit register is to put 8 of these bad boys next to each other.

USB Transceiver

The ASIC design lab class I took was one of the most challenging but also rewarding classes I have ever taken. Did it suck all of the free time out of my life? Yes. But the feeling of making a really good Systemverilog testbench to test a module that you created with a very thorough and detailed RTL diagram and see it all work (after debugging of course) really made it feel worth it. That or its Stockholm syndrome take your pick

The final project for this course was a collaborative project where I teamed up with two other students and designed an AHB-lite enabled USB Transceiver. My specific portion of the project was the AHB subordinate device, responsible for communicating with an SoC to operate the transceiver. Unfortunately, I cannot show my RTL, testbench, module, state diagram, or waveforms for academic integrity reasons but I assure you they were beautiful.

Since I cannot show anything, I will instead recap one design challenge that I encountered and how I solved it. In the end, my subordinate device was able to transfer data to and from the USB transceiver, and the USB data was stored in a FIFO data buffer that could only communicate 1 byte to/from my device per cycle. I had to design a finite state machine to handle reads/writes of greater than 1 byte from the buffer. My initial solution was to try to stall the address phase of the transaction (hready low), load the bytes into an intermediate register, then allow the data phase to happen once the data was available on hrdata. Unfortunately, this method contains an oversight in that I couldn't (reliably) pull hready low before the data phase. I had to stall the data phase, and due to the pipelined nature of AHB, that meant that I needed to register the old address, size, etc of the transaction because they are replaced by the information of the next transaction. So, I did just that. I registered the inputs, and if the address of a transaction indicated that it was a buffer operation, my device would use those registered inputs rather than the current one.

What this project did for me was demystify the process of how computers are designed. Its always very easy to assume that its magic performed by someone smarter than you, but understanding even a smaller ASIC like this at the register level makes the whole ordeal seem more acheivable.

Smart Home System

In my microcontrollers class, I had a final project that required me to make a device using a STM32 microcontroller. My group decided to make a prototype for a smart home system, complete with an alarm, thermostat, and an LCD display to communicate to the user any important information. The alarm system could be enabled and disabled with a keypad, and was set off by a hall effect sensor. If the alarm was triggered, a buzzer utilizing the microcontroller's PWM would sound. The thermostat consisted of a temperature sensor and a motor that served as a mock AC system. The temperature sensor was read with an ADC, and if the current temperature was higher than the target temperature (specified by the user with the keypad), the motor would turn on, as you would expect an automatic AC to work. Lastly, we used an SPI-enabled LCD display to display the alarm state, current temperature, and target temperature to the user.

Multi-Box V2

At my first internship with Wavelogix, my main project was to create a "Multi-Box" that could operate up to 12 of the company's innovative concrete strength sensors at once in a lab testing scenario. This was my first step into the world of embedded systems, and I am still grateful for what I learned from it. It was this project that had me design and solder my first ever PCB using KiCad. Unfortunately the internship ended before we could finish the web app, but most of the physical box was completed.

Radiation Effects Testing PCBs

In the summer of 2024 I interned with Reliable Microsystems. At Rel-Micro, I was a part of the PCB design team, whose job was to create the large circuit boards that the company uses to test its radiation hardened ASICs in lab scenarios. While there, I participated in the design of 2 such PCBs, from start to finish. I made diagrams to map out the signals on the die pad of the chip, as well as documented all of my changes for future reference. Along with my team, we participated in multiple quality review meetings with the higher-ups of the company to ensure the PCB had no issues and minimal hazards such as parasitic capacitance/inductance, as these can be detrimental in a radiation testing environment.

After completing this job, I feel like I am industry ready in terms of PCB design skills. I know KiCad like the back of my hand now, and am certain that I could pick up any other PCB software very quick thanks to my experience at Rel-Micro. Another software that I was introduced to at Rel-Micro was Cadence Virtuoso IC design software. While none of the large projects I worked on there involved Cadence, I was exposed to the software and got to design and run simulations on a small transistor level schematic that I made.
